<p><b>GW190521</b> (or <b>GW190521g</b>; initially, <b>S190521g</b>)<sup id="cite_ref-PRL-20200902_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PRL-20200902-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> is a <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_wave" title="Gravitational wave">gravitational wave</a> signal resulting from the merger of two <a href="/wiki/Black_hole" title="Black hole">black holes</a>. It was possibly associated with a coincident flash of light; if this association is correct, the merger would have occurred near a third <a href="/wiki/Supermassive_black_hole" title="Supermassive black hole">supermassive black hole</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-NASA-20200625_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-20200625-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NYT-20200625_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20200625-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The event was observed by the <a href="/wiki/LIGO" title="LIGO">LIGO</a> and <a href="/wiki/Virgo_interferometer" title="Virgo interferometer">Virgo</a> detectors on 21 May 2019 at 03:02:29 UTC,<sup id="cite_ref-UL-2020_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UL-2020-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> and published on 2 September 2020.<sup id="cite_ref-AJ-20200902_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AJ-20200902-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-PRL-20200902_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PRL-20200902-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-LIGO-20200902_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LIGO-20200902-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The event was 17&#160;billion light years away,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;n 1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-PRL-20200902_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PRL-20200902-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NYT-20200903_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20200903-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> within a 765 deg<sup>2</sup> area<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;n 2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NASA-20190521_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-20190521-12">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> towards <a href="/wiki/Coma_Berenices" title="Coma Berenices">Coma Berenices</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canes_Venatici" title="Canes Venatici">Canes Venatici</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Phoenix_(constellation)" title="Phoenix (constellation)">Phoenix</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-LIGO-20190521_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LIGO-20190521-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NASA-20200625_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-20200625-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NYT-20200625_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20200625-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-PHYS-20200625_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PHYS-20200625-13">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>At 85 and 66 <a href="/wiki/Solar_masses" class="mw-redirect" title="Solar masses">solar masses</a> (<var>M</var><sub>☉</sub>) respectively, the two black holes comprising this merger are the largest progenitor masses observed to date.<sup id="cite_ref-FRBS-20200903_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FRBS-20200903-14">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The resulting black hole had a mass equivalent to 142 times that of the Sun, making this the first clear detection of an <a href="/wiki/Intermediate-mass_black_hole" title="Intermediate-mass black hole">intermediate-mass black hole</a>. The remaining 9 solar masses were radiated as energy in the form of gravitational waves.<sup id="cite_ref-AJ-20200902_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AJ-20200902-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-PRL-20200902_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PRL-20200902-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-LIGO-20200902_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LIGO-20200902-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_significance">Physical significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=GW190521&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Physical significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>GW190521 is a significant discovery due to the masses of the resulting large black hole, and one or both of the smaller constituent black holes. Stellar evolution theory predicts that stars cannot produce black holes of more than about 65&#160;<var>M</var><sub>&#x2609;</sub>, so there should be a <a href="/wiki/Stellar_black_hole#Upper_mass_gap" title="Stellar black hole">black hole mass gap</a> above that value. The <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7001850000000000000♠"></span>85<span style="margin-left:0.3em;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:85%;text-align:right;">+21<br />−14</span></span></span>&#160;<var>M</var><sub>&#x2609;</sub> and <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7002142000000000000♠"></span>142<span style="margin-left:0.3em;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:85%;text-align:right;">+28<br />−16</span></span></span>&#160;<var>M</var><sub>&#x2609;</sub> black holes observed in GW190521 are conclusively in the mass gap, indicating that it can be populated by the mergers of smaller black holes.<sup id="cite_ref-AJ-20200902_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AJ-20200902-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Only indirect evidence for <a href="/wiki/Intermediate-mass_black_hole" title="Intermediate-mass black hole">intermediate mass black holes</a>, those with between 100 and 100,000 <a href="/wiki/Solar_mass" title="Solar mass">solar masses</a>, had been observed earlier, and it was unclear how they had formed.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Researchers hypothesize "a hierarchical merger, in which the two progenitor black holes themselves may have formed from the merging of two smaller black holes, before migrating together and eventually merging."<sup id="cite_ref-LIGO-20200902_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LIGO-20200902-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>According to discovery team member <a href="/wiki/Vicky_Kalogera" title="Vicky Kalogera">Vassiliki Kalogera</a> of <a href="/wiki/Northwestern_University" title="Northwestern University">Northwestern University</a>, "this is the first and only firm/secure mass measurement of an intermediate mass black hole at the time of its birth ... Now we know reliably at least one way [such objects can form], through the merger of other black holes."<sup id="cite_ref-NYT-20200903_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20200903-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Possible_electromagnetic_counterpart">Possible electromagnetic counterpart</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=GW190521&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Possible electromagnetic counterpart">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In June 2020 astronomers reported observations of a flash of light that might be associated with GW190521. The <a href="/wiki/Zwicky_Transient_Facility" title="Zwicky Transient Facility">Zwicky Transient Facility</a> (ZTF) reported a transient optical source within the region of the GW190521g trigger, though as the uncertainty in sky position was hundreds of square degrees the association remains uncertain. If the two events are actually linked, the event is claimed to be the first finding of an <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic source</a> related to the merger of two black holes.<sup id="cite_ref-NASA-20200625_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-20200625-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-PRL-20200625_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PRL-20200625-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NYT-20200625_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20200625-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SD-20200626_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SD-20200626-16">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Mergers of black holes do not typically emit any light. The researchers suggest that it could be explained if the merging of the two smaller black holes sent the newly formed intermediate mass black hole on a trajectory that hurtled through the <a href="/wiki/Accretion_disk" title="Accretion disk">accretion disk</a> of an unrelated but nearby <a href="/wiki/Supermassive_black_hole" title="Supermassive black hole">supermassive black hole</a>, disrupting the disk material and producing a flare of light. The newly formed black hole would have traveled at 200&#160;km/s (120&#160;mi/s) through the disk, according to the astronomers.<sup id="cite_ref-IFLS-20200625_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IFLS-20200625-17">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> If this explanation is correct, the flare should repeat after about 1.6 years<sup id="cite_ref-PRL-20200625_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PRL-20200625-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> when the intermediate mass black hole again encounters the accretion disk.<sup id="cite_ref-IFLS-20200625_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IFLS-20200625-17">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>According to Matthew Graham, lead astronomer for the study, "This supermassive black hole was burbling along for years before this more abrupt flare. The flare occurred on the right timescale, and in the right location, to be coincident with the gravitational-wave event. In our study, we conclude that the flare is likely the result of a black hole merger, but we cannot completely rule out other possibilities."<sup id="cite_ref-IFLS-20200625_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IFLS-20200625-17">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
